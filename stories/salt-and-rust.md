# Salt and Rust

The tanker had been a tanker once. Now it was a island, and the island was on wheels, and the wheels were sunk to their hubs in wet salt.

Furiosa walked the deck barefoot. The metal still held the night's cold. Below her, on the flats, the rig's six tractors lay tilted where the crust had given way, their tires drowned. Two days. Maybe three before the tide came back and took the rest.

She had not known the sea could do that. Come and go. The old maps in the Citadel had shown blue, but blue had meant nothing to her until she drove west past the last of the red and found water that breathed.

A gull screamed. She watched it bank and drop toward something in the shallows.

Then the something stood up.

He came out of the water slow, the way a thing comes out that has been waiting. Tall. Long hair plastered. A harpoon across his back and a knife at his hip that had been cut from a propeller blade. He stopped a hundred meters out and looked at her rig the way a man looks at a fire he is deciding whether to sit by.

She raised the sawn-off so he could see it. He raised one open hand. Then he came on.

Up close he smelled of brine and something else, sharper. He had gills. Three slits behind each ear, pale and closed now, fluttering when he breathed.

"Your wheels are stuck," he said.

"I noticed."

"Tide's coming." He looked past her at the rig. "Six hours. Maybe five."

"I have men working."

"You have three men working and one of them is dying." He had not looked at the work crew. He had heard them. The wet cough from the man under the third axle. "Lung rot. He won't see morning."

Furiosa did not lower the gun. "What do you want."

"Passage. East. Off the salt."

"Why."

He thought about it. That was the first thing she liked about him, that he thought about it instead of lying fast.

"Smokers took my boat," he said. "I've been walking nine days. I can't drink this." He tipped his head at the flats. "And I can drink most things."

---

She put him to work. He did not complain about it and he did not thank her for it, which were the second and third things.

He knew water. That was the use of him. He went out across the crust on a plank he cut from the wheelhouse roof, distributing his weight, and he found them three soaks where the salt was thinnest and the freshwater pushed up from below. He filled their skins. He showed the boy Toast how to taste for the iron that meant a soak was about to fail.

The dying man died at noon. They put him in the salt and the salt took him fast, the way it takes everything.

"You knew," Furiosa said.

"I know lungs."

"You a medic."

"I was a lot of things."

She watched him coil rope. His hands did it without him watching, the loops going down even and tight. Sailor's hands. She had seen hands like that exactly never, because where she came from no one had ever had cause to coil a rope around anything but a throat.

"What do they call you," she said.

He looked at the horizon a while.

"Mariner," he said, like he was trying it on. "It'll do."

---

The Smokers came at dusk.

She heard them before she saw them: the wrong sound, two-stroke engines tuned mean, coming in low across the flats on something that was not a car and not a boat. Skiffs. Skiffs with wheels welded under the hulls and sails up to catch the evening wind, skating across the crust at forty kilometers an hour because they weighed nothing and drew nothing and did not care that the salt would not hold a truck.

"Eight," the Mariner said. He was already moving. "Maybe nine. The big one in front is Deacon's."

"Friends of yours."

"He's the one took my boat."

Furiosa was up the ladder to the tanker's spine before he finished the sentence. She had the long rifle out of its oilcloth and the bipod down on the rail and the first round chambered by the time the lead skiff was inside two hundred meters.

She missed the helmsman and hit the mast. The mast was wood and old and it came apart in a way wood is not supposed to, splintering down its length, and the sail collapsed across the deck and the skiff slewed and went over and the crust opened under it and ate it whole. Two men screamed going down. The salt closed.

"Good shot," the Mariner said, climbing up beside her with the harpoon.

"I was aiming at the helmsman."

"Still good."

The other skiffs broke formation. They had not expected the rig to shoot back at that range. One peeled left, trying to get behind the tanker where her angle would die. The Mariner watched it come.

"Can you hold the others?"

"For a minute."

"A minute is enough."

He went over the rail. Not down the ladder. Over. He hit the salt twelve meters below in a runner's crouch and was up and sprinting before she registered he had jumped. The harpoon was off his back and in his hand. He ran straight at the flanking skiff, into its line, and the helmsman laughed and steered to run him down.

The Mariner went under the bow.

Not around. Under. He dropped flat at the last second and the skiff passed over him and he came up behind it with one hand on the rudder post and the harpoon went up into the helmsman's back through the kidney and out through the sternum and the helmsman folded over the tiller and the skiff carved hard to starboard and into its own wake-line of broken crust. It went down stern-first. The Mariner was already swimming. Actually swimming, in six inches of brine over hardpan, the way a lizard swims across sand.

Furiosa shot the mast out of the third skiff and the fourth turned and ran.

By the time the sun was fully down there were four wrecks on the flats and the gulls had come and the Mariner was walking back to the rig with something in his hand. He climbed the ladder and dropped it on the deck at her feet.

A compass. Brass. Old-world. The needle still swung.

"Deacon's," he said.

"He on that skiff."

"No. But he'll come for this." The Mariner wiped his harpoon on a rag. "So we should be elsewhere."

---

They got the rig out at moonrise. The Mariner had found, in his walking on the planks, a ridge of harder salt running northeast like a road laid down by something that had known there would one day be a road needed. They winched the tractors over to it one by one. It took four hours. The tide came in at the end and took the last tractor's rear wheels and they drove out of the rising water with the salt hissing against the hot exhaust.

On the high ground she stopped the rig and they looked back. The flats were a sheet of black water now under the moon. No sign of the wrecks. No sign there had ever been wrecks, or skiffs, or men.

"Where east," he said.

"There's green country. Three weeks' drive. We're taking seed there."

"Seed."

"To plant."

He was quiet a long time.

"I had a map once," he said. "Showed a place. Dry land all the way around, but green. I spent twenty years looking."

"Did you find it."

"I found a lot of places that weren't it."

She thought about that.

"Ride with us," she said. "To the green country. Then decide."

He looked at the compass in his hand. The needle had settled. It was pointing the way they were already going, which meant nothing, or meant everything, depending on what kind of person you were about needles.

"All right," he said.

She put the rig in gear. The salt fell away behind them and did not follow.
